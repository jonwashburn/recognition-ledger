\section{Introduction}

The nature of gravity on cosmic scales remains one of the most profound mysteries in physics. Despite increasingly sensitive experiments \cite{Aalbers2023,Hu2024,Du2022}, no direct detection of dark matter particles has been achieved. Meanwhile, the tight empirical relationships between baryonic matter and gravitational anomalies---such as the radial acceleration relation (RAR) \cite{McGaugh2016,Lelli2017}, baryonic Tully-Fisher relation (BTFR) \cite{Lelli2019,Goddy2023}, and mass-discrepancy acceleration relation (MDAR) \cite{McGaugh2004,DiCintio2015}---suggest that the missing gravity problem may be fundamentally geometric rather than material.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Theoretical incompleteness}: No unique interpolating function $\mu(x)$; multiple empirical forms work equally well

\item \textbf{Cluster problems}: Requires additional dark matter or neutrinos to explain galaxy clusters \cite{Angus2008}
\item \textbf{No fundamental origin}: The acceleration scale $a_0$ appears ad hoc without deeper justification
\end{enumerate}
